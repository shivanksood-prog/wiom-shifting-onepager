The real story: confirmation quality went up, not down. Customer-confirmed shifting rose steadily 23% → 35%. What collapsed was ping-based closure (29% → 1%) — the team stopped closing tickets on a router ping alone. Headline "shifted" falls from 52% to 36% only because the weaker evidence standard was withdrawn.
But the cases that used to be absorbed by ping now have nowhere to go. Open/unreachable climbed to 40% and denied/delayed to 20%. Removing the shortcut exposed demand the calling process cannot close — it did not fix it.

9 of June's 547 requesters had no resolvable install date and are excluded. Active base uses the standard Active Customers (15D) definition — plan active within 15 days of the reference date. Cross-checked against the canonical active_customers_r15 metric, which returns 97,623 for the same date — a 0.4% gap. The canonical source is aggregated at partner grain and cannot be split by tenure, so the customer-level rebuild here is what the split requires; the 0.4% variance does not move any rate by more than 0.1 per 1,000.

Roughly 6 in every 1,000 active customers ask to shift each month — and that holds at every tenure. The rate runs 5.5 per 1,000 overall and no cohort strays far from it: the lowest is 24-month customers at 4.8, the highest is 3–6 month customers at 8.5. Shifting is a broad-base behaviour, not a new-customer problem, and there is no tenure segment worth targeting separately.
Measured against the total installed base instead, the same data shows a 6× new-customer skew — that version is an artifact and should not be used. The installed base carries a large tail of long-lapsed mature customers who inflate the 12-24 and 24+ month denominators. Once those are removed, the skew mostly disappears. Denominator choice changes this conclusion more than anything else on the page.
The one number that stands out is a success rate, not a volume. 3–6 month customers complete their shift only 26% of the time, against 41–46% for every other cohort. On 43 cases this is suggestive rather than conclusive, but it is the only cohort that breaks the otherwise flat pattern and is worth a look at the underlying cases.

What this adds up to

  • Only ~42% of shifting requests end in a verified shift. The largest single failure mode is the customer becoming unreachable after raising the request (DNP) — now 147 cases a month and rising while volume falls.
  • Withdrawing ping-based closure was the right call, but it was a measurement change, not a fix. Genuine customer-confirmed shifts rose 23%→35%. The demand that ping used to absorb is now visible as open and denied cases.
  • The manual sheet is the bottleneck. Confirmation, eligibility and payout are three independently-maintained columns, and they disagree with each other. June's payout is still only two-thirds unpaid seven weeks on; July's has not started.
  • Partners are financing the delay. ₹46.3k across 260 payments is outstanding — money owed for work already completed in the field.
  • Tenure is not the story. Roughly 6 in every 1,000 active customers ask to shift each month, and that rate holds at every tenure. The 6× new-customer skew appears only if you use the total installed base as the denominator, which is inflated by long-lapsed customers. One genuine outlier: 3–6 month customers complete their shift just 26% of the time vs 41–46% everywhere else.
Method & caveats. Unique customers counted on mobile number, deduplicated within each month; a customer requesting in two months counts once in each. Status buckets were normalised across months — the tracker's sub-status vocabulary changed in June (a "Resolved –" prefix was introduced) and June/July taxonomies differ from April/May. July covers 1–19 July only; monthly comparisons of outcome mix use a like-for-like cohort of tickets opened in days 1–12 so every month has had equal time to close. TAT is open-date to closing-date in calendar days. Tenure joined from Snowflake on mobile number at a 98.6% match rate (33 of 2,370 unmatched), tenure defined as first install / first router mapping, so a reinstalled customer shows their original start date rather than their current stint. The tenure view is June only and uses Active Customers (15D) as the denominator (98,002 as of 30 June), with tenure measured at the same reference date. Install date is the earliest of the tenure dimension and first router mapping. The tracker is city-limited while the active base is all-India, so absolute per-1,000 rates are understated; the relative ordering across cohorts holds only if tenure mix is similar across cities, which has not been verified. Payment figures are as recorded in the tracker, which is not reconciled against Finance disbursement.
